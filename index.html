Women's Health & COVID-19

Healthcare services have experienced significant disruptions and backlogs as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic all over the world, including the United States.

Like many other segments of the population, women are bearing the impact of these disruptions in significant ways.

Particularly non-emergency services, such as regular gynecological check-ups, testing and screening for sexually transmitted infections and breast cancer, and elective procedures, have been significantly delayed.

These delays have left millions of women across the United States without primary care and routine immunization. This has, in turn, increased the risk of common infections and noncommunicable diseases in women.

According to a survey done by the World Health Organization in 21 African countries following the pandemic, it was found that [3];

  • The use of contraceptives has significantly decreased in 48% of countries
  • There has been a rise in teenage pregnancies by 60% since the beginning of the pandemic
  • Poverty rates have increased from 11.7% to 12.5% in just two years

We can see from the above stats that while death rates from COVID-19 are higher among men [11], women are more likely to experience the indirect health consequences of the pandemic.

How Does COVID-19 Affect Women's Health?

Women's health encompasses many areas of healthcare, including;

  • Sexual & reproductive health
  • Prenatal care
  • Menopause & hormone therapy
  • Breast cancer and ovarian cancer
  • Women and heart disease

In addition, because of losing their jobs, facing economic hardships, and being victims of domestic violence more than ever, women are also suffering from severe mental health disorders all over the world.

Due to the pandemic, there has been a disruption in access to reproductive health services, such as family planning and abortion.

All these matters combined can have long-lasting adverse effects on women's health.

Sexual & Reproductive Health

A few studies have been conducted since 2020 that indicate a connection between female sexual health and the pandemic.

Social distancing and other restrictions imposed to contain the virus resulted in;

  • reduced sexual activity,
  • higher rates of sexual dysfunction in females, and
  • increased stress and anxiety

It was also found that this change in sexual activity was more pronounced in women than in men who have contracted the virus.

The leading cause for an increase in sexual dysfunction during the pandemic is the psychological strain that women are experiencing throughout the world. Economic problems and reduced sports activities have also led to this issue, particularly in females.

According to published literature [7], people working in the healthcare sector have suffered these problems even more than those working in other occupations.

The following reasons have been highlighted for this situation

Excessive fatigue and stress

Fear of getting sick

High workload

There is a need to provide adequate healthcare facilities to females who are COVID-19 survivors for sexual counseling and the long-term effects of the pandemic on their sexual health.

Prenatal Care During COVID-19

Pregnant women experience a multitude of physical and emotional changes during the prenatal period.

These changes are often accompanied by life stressors that can further complicate the experience. However, the COVID-19 pandemic has added an extra layer of stress and uncertainty for pregnant women.

It is essential for pregnant women to take care of their mental health during these uncertain times and particularly if they're pregnant. This is because stress and anxiety can have adverse effects on both the mother and the baby.

Women are advised to seek virtual prenatal care during COVID-19 in order to minimize the risk of contracting the virus.

Here are a few things you can do during the prenatal period to increase your chances of delivering a healthy baby;

  • Book regular virtual appointments with your ob-gyn team
  • Visit a local hospital for ultrasounds when needed
  • Watch out for warning signs at home
  • Monitor your blood pressure, weight, and blood sugar level
  • Get tested for COVID-19 before delivery

Cases of Sexual/Domestic Violence in the Wake of COVID-19

With limited access to healthcare and support systems during the pandemic, there is an alarming rise in sexual and domestic violence cases following the pandemic.

Lockdowns and economic uncertainties have further exacerbated the situation.

According to the WHO, in 2021, 245 million women and girls were reportedly sexually/physically abused annually [3].

It is a sad reality that the majority of cases involving violence against women have been committed by their partners or spouses, rather than by experienced criminals.

The WHO has also reported a decline in healthcare services extended to women who have been victims of domestic violence in 56% of African countries.

As the COVID-19 pandemic continues to spread worldwide, it is important for women to take necessary precautions to protect themselves from the consequences of this pandemic.

Moreover, healthcare providers must ensure that women have adequate and timely access to all services, particularly regarding women-specific health problems.

COVID-19 and Pregnancy

While research on the topic is ongoing, some evidence suggests that pregnant women are at a higher risk of developing COVID-related complications than normal people [2].

There is also a possibility that COVID-19 may be transmitted to the baby during pregnancy or childbirth.

However, the good news is such cases have been very rarely reported, and therefore, babies born to pregnant women who have been sick with COVID-19 during their pregnancy are not exposed to any severe health issues.

Having said that, there has been some research that suggests that pregnant women with the coronavirus infection are more likely to;

  • Deliver prematurely,
  • Have a miscarriage, and
  • Have a stillbirth

To prevent these risks, pregnant women are advised to take extra precautions during the pandemic.

It is also important to note that the Black and Hispanic population is more likely to be affected by COVID-19 when pregnant [2].

To protect themselves and their babies, Black and Hispanic women are advised to get vaccinated as soon as possible.

What To Do If You're Pregnant and Get COVID-19?

First of all, you need to be watchful of the following symptoms when you're pregnant or have recently given birth;

  • Chills and fever
  • Muscle pains
  • A loss of taste or smell
  • Cough and sore throat
  • Headache

If you experience these symptoms, you must get in touch with your healthcare provider right away.

Doctors usually recommend a monoclonal antibody medication to pregnant women if they get infected by the coronavirus. This treatment involves a single infusion of a monoclonal antibody medicine intravenously.

It is recommended to contact your doctor right after you experience COVID-19 symptoms because this medication is the most effective when given at the start of the infection.

The CDC recommends that postpartum women take precautionary measures seriously to protect themselves from the virus, such as wearing masks, practicing social distancing, and washing hands frequently.

This is because postpartum women may be more susceptible to severe illness from COVID-19 due to changes in the immune system and respiratory function during pregnancy and postpartum recovery [9].

Pregnant women may also be advised to take safe pain-relieving medicine during this time period. They must rest completely and take plenty of fluids to keep their electrolyte levels in check.

Vaccination in Pregnancy

According to the CDC, it is perfectly safe for women to get vaccinated while pregnant or breastfeeding [4].

In fact, getting vaccinated if you're pregnant is highly recommended because research shows pregnant women may experience severe COVID-19 symptoms if they contract the virus.

It's a common misconception that COVID-19 may cause fertility problems in men and women.

Some theories have also been circulating since 2020 that COVID-19 vaccines may cause early pregnancy loss.

Although these beliefs are not backed by scientific proof or study results, people may start believing them out of fear or uncertainty.

A study conducted in 2022 [5] has confirmed that there is no association between fertility problems and COVID-19 vaccines.

By getting vaccinated, both the mother and the baby can be protected from the virus.

Changes in State-Funded Medicaid Program After May 2023

With the expiration of the COVID-19 public health emergency (PHE) in May 2023, it has been estimated that up to 18 million people could potentially lose Medicaid coverage.

While this figure itself is alarming, recent surveys show that approximately 3.8 million people in the country would be uninsured after disenrollment from Medicaid and re-enrollment to other programs like CHIP [10].

This means that a significant portion of the population may not have access to free COVID tests, vaccines, and hospital care once the PHE ends in May.

The above stats raise concerns about potential impact of PHE expiration on vulnerable populations, particularly those with chronic health conditions or disabilities.

Here is an overview of a few changes that are expected to take place for uninsured and underinsured people after the PHE ends in May 2023;

COVID-19 Vaccines

People who have Medicare or Medicaid insurance will continue to receive free vaccines even after the PHE ends. However, uninsured people will no longer be able to access free vaccines following this decision.

This means that those without insurance will have to find alternative means to obtain the vaccine, such as by purchasing it themselves or seeking out community health clinics.

HCPSC holds free vaccination events from time to time. Visit link to learn about the latest vaccination events in your area.

COVID-19 Tests

When COVID-19 was declared a public and national health emergency back in 2020, free tests were made available to the general public via emergency funding.

If you have Medicare insurance, you can still order up to 8 at-home COVID-19 tests. However, people who were enrolled in Medicaid during the pandemic will be required to purchase these tests from their pocket if their physician advises them.

According to the Kaiser Family Foundation (KFF), the cost of these tests will vary according to an individual's insurance status.

COVID-19 Treatments

COVID-19 treatment costs are probably going to see the biggest transition after the PHE expires. This transition would not only result in changes to the cost of COVID-19 treatment, but also to the way that healthcare providers are reimbursed for their services.

Medicare beneficiaries will continue to see some sort of cost sharing for the treatments. In addition, people covered under Medicaid programs will be reimbursed for their COVID treatments for a year after the PHE ends

After June 2024, only those treatments will be covered by Medicaid that have been approved by the FDA. People with private insurance will also experience cost sharing and higher premiums after June 2024.

All in all, people who already have limited access to healthcare resources are going to face the biggest challenge once the PHE ends in May 2023. This includes a significant decrease in

telehealth services as well as the end of the Title 42 border policy. This means that migrants who had been blocked earlier to enter the US will now be allowed to enter now.

It is important to keep in mind that the end of the public health emergency surrounding COVID-19 does not mean that the virus has also been eradicated completely.

Although vaccination efforts have significantly reduced the spread of COVID-19 and the number of hospitalizations and deaths associated with it, there is still a risk of emergency situations arising due to the virus.

When Is It Important to Seek Medical Help?

It is important for women to be proactive about seeking medical help when needed.

Here are a few instances that women must never overlook, mainly if they are pregnant, at a high risk of developing COVID-related complications, and have an underlying health condition, such as diabetes.

Regular gynecological check-ups

These are important for the early detection and prevention of potential health issues related to women's reproductive health. Delaying or skipping these checkups due to fear of contracting COVID-19 could lead to further complications or missed opportunities for treatment.

Having unusual symptoms

If you experience shortness of breath, fever, chest pain, or anything else that seems unusual and out of place, get tested for COVID-19 immediately. It is also important to discuss these symptoms with your healthcare provider, so they can suggest a treatment early on.

Cancer screening

Another instance when women must not overlook consulting their healthcare provider is for regular breast cancer screening. Your physician may advise breast self-exam, MRI, or ultrasound depending on any unusual symptoms and devise a treatment plan accordingly.

Screening for STIs

Regular screening for sexually transmitted infections should be part of every woman's preventive care, especially during the pandemic. This is important to avoid a misdiagnosis and delayed treatment and to prevent the spread of STIs during COVID-19.

Family Planning

The use of contraceptives has significantly changed during the COVID-19 pandemic. This is mainly true for hormonal contraceptives for women that are accessible only with a prescription as well as implant and intrauterine contraceptive devices [8].

If you want to book a face-to-face contraceptive consultation or discuss your preferred choice of family planning with a specialist, visit HCPSC.

It is particularly important to consult a healthcare professional for family planning if you have had a baby in the last six months and also have an underlying health condition, such as diabetes or breast cancer.

Prenatal & Labor Care

The COVID-19 pandemic has brought to light the importance of comprehensive maternity services for the health and well-being of mothers and their infants.

In addition to quality prenatal care, safe delivery facilities must also be made available to ensure all complications are handled properly and there is absolutely minimal risk of mortality.

At HCPSC, the specialized maternity services also include screening for COVID-19 and continuous monitoring of the mother's health along with family planning advice.

Postpartum Care

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) has reported that mothers who have recently given birth are at a particularly high risk of experiencing severe complications from COVID-19 [9].

In order to protect themselves from getting sick, mothers must receive extensive and high-quality postpartum care.

Severe illness during the postpartum period may require the mother to be hospitalized, admitted to an ICU, or put on a ventilator. Being older than 25 years, having an underlying health condition like diabetes, and being Hispanic or Black [2] may increase your risk of getting severely ill from COVID-19.

Final Thoughts

The pandemic has led to the postponement or cancellation of routine and emergency medical procedures, leading to delayed diagnoses and treatment.

It has particularly been challenging for women as they have difficulty accessing quality and timely healthcare. Delayed prenatal and postnatal care have had severe consequences for both mother and child.

Additionally, the pandemic has had a disproportionate economic impact on women, many of whom work in healthcare or other essential industries, increasing the burden of the pandemic on their physical and mental health.

As the world continues to grapple with the effects of COVID-19, it is crucial to prioritize the provision of essential health services for women and ensure that they receive the care they need to maintain their health and well-being.
